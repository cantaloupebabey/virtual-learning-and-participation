The Question

How does participation influence completion and achievment in the virtual classroom?

By using Marlon Ferrari's "E-Learning Student Reactions" dataset from Kaggle, we could see on a small scale how factors such as participation (by number of posts and time spent online) and well as how many reactions each student recieved.

The Process

What Steps were taken?

Clean up

First, I had to remove absent students. Slackers. Using df.drop, I deleted rows (students) if their time online was 0 seconds.

Whoever thought it was a smart idea to use commas instead of a period to separate demical values CHOSE to make my life harder. I used x.replace in a for loop to change all ',' to '.'


Fixing + Adding columns

I also turned values for time online into minutes from seconds with another for loop, so it would be easier to interpret the information.

After fixing all the individual sk grade values, it was a breeze using df.iloc to average the students' grades into a new column.

I also made a total reactions column, by using the same line but with different splicing and .sum instead of .mean

Printing values for Chart JS

With a lot little help from Nicole, I used df.iterrows to df.append x and y coordinates in the specified format to a dictionary, then printed the dictionary. Even though it had extra quote marks, using ctrl+f, ctrl+h, and about 3 minutes, the chart data was done.

Data analysis, conclusion

With the charts mostly done, all was left to do was to actually analyze the data. I wanted to adjust some of the charts to not include student #66 because they seemed to be outside the standard deviation by a lot but I left them because they still serve to be a good real-life example to reference.

I mostly used clustering to deduce and interpret the information, since the passing colors made it clear there were two different groups.

What does the data reveal?

Mean posts per student: 5.5 posts
Mean time online per student: 171.3 minutes (2.9 hours)
Percent of students approved after the class: 70.4%

Note: In the charts below, red = students that did not pass the class, and green = students that passed.


This first chart graphs time spent on the virtual platform to the number of posts; it's a pretty strightforward chart. As the time spent online increases, so does the number of posts. Some students on the right side of the chart (and student #66 who was super active) are outliers, but the general trend shows that time and number of posts have a positive correlation.



Chart 2 illustrates time spent online to the final grade of each student. This time, we see a positive correlation, as people who spent more time in the virtual classroom had higher final grades. While there were people who didn't have to spent much time to earn a high grade, there wasn't anyone who spent >128 minutes that failed the class.



We know that time and number of posts are positively correlated, but I still thought it was a good idea to do a number of posts to final grade chart. As we can see here, it's also positively correlated. The chart looks a little skewed since student number 66 posted a lot but the grade value is limited. However, it's important to note that the biggest cluster around (5, 7) shows that many people who perhaps posted more moderately were also able to pass the class.



These final charts illustrate the relationship between number of reactions recieved to final grades and number of posts to number of reactions recieved - measuring the correlation between peer reciprocation on grades I hypothesized that grades are impacted by individual participation, but I would like to accurately represent all the the data given.
Reactions recieved and grade have a positive correlation, but if you compare the number of posts v. final grade chart (chart 3), they look almost identical. Additionally, the more posts posted by a student, the more reactions they will recieve.


Conclusion

While the data shows that people with high participation always passed the class, clusters in the graphs show that it does not necessarily mean that people with low participation always failed. In fact, there were more than several people who failed the class that participated more than people who passed.

While a pre-test in the original dataset would be necessary to make the most accurate predictions, my theory is that people who were mid-low effort but still passed the course would have gotten a high rating beforehand, while low effort-low grade subjects did not grow from their initially low-scoring state.

So what does this mean for students learning online? It may not be necessary to overexert yourself over assignments or posting to the class stream super often (student #66, I'm looking at you), be aware of your abilities in current standing to determine how much you need to learn. Despite the data, it is still advisable to students to try their best during class time and on assignments :)

Something I would change about the procedure of this project is to use multiple linear regression or a function that would allow me to combine time online and number of posts variables - causation would be clearer.
